Aspirated consonants

Erli has four consonants that come with a strong puff of breath right after them. They're written as digraphs ending in h (with one exception, čš). The puff is meaningful — it can change the word.

LetterHow it soundsMinimal pair
khlike k, with extra breath — a little like the k in English kit when said emphaticallykher "house"  vs.  ker! "make!"
phlike p, with extra breath — not like English ph in "phone" (which is just /f/)pheràv "I fill"  vs.  peràv "I fall"
thlike t, with extra breath — not like English th in "this" or "thin"thud "milk"  vs.  tut "you (acc.)"
čšlike č (English ch) followed by a clear š — "church + sh" run togetherčšurì "knife"  vs.  čurì "braid"

In other Romani varieties the same sound as čš is usually written čh. The Sofia Erli spelling reflects that here the aspiration is pronounced as an audible š instead of just a puff.

A note on word-final sounds

At the end of a word, voiced consonants are pronounced as their voiceless partners — the same as in Bulgarian:

  • thud "milk" is said thut
  • jag "fire" is said jak
  • diz "city" is said dis
  • thuv "smoke" is said thuf

The voicing comes back as soon as a vowel-initial ending follows: jagà "fires", dizà "cities".

The archaic ř

Old Sofia Erli had a separate cerebral r (tongue curled back, touching the palate) that contrasted with the regular r. Today it survives in only a handful of words — ařò "egg", hařò "bow / sword", poř "intestine". In everyday speech it's mostly merged with the regular r.

Stress

Inherited Erli words almost always carry the stress on the last syllable:

  • čšavò "boy", gilì "song", kalò "black"

Loanwords from Bulgarian usually keep their original stress (slùga "servant", òčila "spectacles"). Stress can distinguish words: kàna? "when?" vs. kanà "ears".
